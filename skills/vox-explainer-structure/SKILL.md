# The Vox-style explainer structure

A short explainer video is not a lecture compressed in time. It's a *narrative* that smuggles understanding past the viewer's defenses. The Vox/Kurzgesagt/3Blue1Brown pattern has five beats. Skip one and the video falls apart.

## The 5 beats

### 1. Hook (5–10 seconds)
A question, a paradox, or a surprising claim. Never start with "Today we'll talk about X." The viewer needs a reason to keep watching before you've earned their trust.

Examples that work:
- "Why does ice float when literally everything else gets denser when it cools?"
- "You can fit a million Earths inside the sun. But that's not the weird part."
- "There's a number bigger than infinity. And another one bigger than *that*."

What kills hooks: announcing the topic, defining the term, "let's explore," "have you ever wondered."

### 2. Tension (10–20 seconds)
Why the obvious answer is wrong or incomplete. This is the pivot from curiosity to *investment*. The viewer now has a stake — they've been shown a gap in their understanding.

The shape: "You'd think X. But actually Y. And that doesn't even explain Z."

### 3. Visual metaphor (30–60 seconds)
The single biggest difference between videos that work and videos that don't. You take the abstract idea and *externalize* it into something spatial, comparative, or animated. The metaphor IS the explanation — narration just labels what's already visible.

Good metaphors:
- Compound interest as a snowball rolling downhill
- Recursion as Russian dolls
- A neural network as a series of voting committees
- Entropy as a shuffled deck

Bad metaphors are ones where the visual is decorative — it sits next to the explanation instead of being the explanation. Test: if you muted the narration, would the metaphor still teach something?

### 4. Reveal (20–40 seconds)
The "ohhh" moment. The metaphor pays off, the tension resolves. This is where the viewer actually *learns*. Pace this beat slowly — give the insight room to land.

### 5. Recap (5–10 seconds)
One sentence the viewer leaves with. If they only remember this, did you do your job? Cut everything that doesn't support this sentence.

## The ONE thing rule

Before drafting any storyboard, write the single sentence the viewer should leave with. Tape it (figuratively) above the monitor. Every beat must serve this sentence. If a beat is interesting but doesn't serve the ONE thing, it's a different video — save it for later.

## Pacing heuristics

- **Total runtime: 90 seconds to 3 minutes** for a single concept. Longer means you have multiple concepts and probably should split.
- **Hook + Tension under 30 seconds.** YouTube retention curves drop steeply in the first 30s.
- **The metaphor is the longest beat.** Don't rush it. The reveal lands only if the metaphor was set up properly.
- **End on a beat, not a fade.** Last word of the recap should land on a frame the viewer can sit with for 1–2 seconds.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | What it looks like | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Wikipedia voice" | Starts with definitions; reads like an article | Open with a question or paradox |
| "Metaphor as garnish" | The visual is pretty but doesn't carry meaning | Make the visual the explanation; cut the verbal explanation |
| "Too many ideas" | Three concepts in two minutes | Pick one. Save the others for sequels |
| "No tension" | "Here's how X works" → list of facts | Find the misconception the viewer holds and dismantle it |
| "Fade-out ending" | Ends with "and that's how X works" | End on the one sentence; let it sit |

## When this skill applies

- Planning any short explainer video, regardless of tool (Manim, Remotion, After Effects, even a slide deck)
- Reviewing a draft storyboard for whether it'll actually land
- Deciding whether a topic is one video or multiple
