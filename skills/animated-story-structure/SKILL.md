# Animated story / book insight structure

An animated story video teaches by making an idea feel like a journey. This is useful for book summaries, philosophy, business lessons, historical anecdotes, and "what this book says" videos.

Do not summarize every chapter. Pick one central claim and animate the chain of reasoning behind it.

## The 5 beats

### 1. Premise
Introduce the surprising idea, dilemma, or promise.

Good:
- "This book argues that optimism is not a mood. It is a method."
- "The character thinks the problem is discipline. The story slowly proves it is feedback."

Avoid starting with author biography unless the author's life is the point of the video.

### 2. World
Set up the world the idea lives in: the character, system, historical moment, or mental model the viewer needs.

For book videos, this is where you explain the author's frame without listing chapters.

### 3. Conflict
Introduce the pressure that makes the idea matter. Something should not fit: a contradiction, failed attempt, moral trade-off, or broken assumption.

The conflict is what prevents the video from becoming a lecture.

### 4. Idea Turn
Reveal the book's or story's central move: the reframe that resolves the conflict.

The shape: "The point is not X. The point is Y."

### 5. Takeaway
End with the practical or conceptual takeaway. Make it specific enough to remember, but not so prescriptive that it distorts the source.

## Adaptation rules

- Preserve the source's central argument; do not manufacture a cleaner lesson than the book supports.
- Use scenes, metaphors, and character actions to carry the idea.
- Name the book or source in `storyboard.md`; include source notes there, not in `narration.txt`.
- If the source has multiple major ideas, ask the user which one the video should focus on.
